Type Advantages Disadvantages Description
Situational Approaches - Able to handle, adapt and react with any situation
- Every level of employee will be managed by different level of leadership behavior
- Create good workplace and creative thinking		 - Suitable for short-term project only
- Require skill set level of the leader		 This is an adaptive leadership style, and it encourages leaders to take stock of their team members, weigh the many variables in their workplace and choose the leadership style that best fits their goals and circumstances.
Skill Approaches - Makes leadership available to everyone by laying emphasis on learned (and learnable) skills rather than on traits
- Provides an expansive view of leadership that incorporates a wide variety of components (ie, problem-solving skills and social judgment skills)		 - Descriptive in nature, it does not explain how skills could lead to effective leadership performance
- Fundamentals appear to be exhaustive, making the approach more generic and less precise		 This approach suggests that skills, knowledge, and abilities are required for a leader to be effective.
Trait Approaches - It is naturally pleasing theory and gives constructive information about leadership
- Managers can utilize the information from the theory to evaluate their position in the organization and to assess how their position can be made stronger in the organization 		- There is bound to be some subjective judgment in determining who is regarded as a 'good' or 'successful' leader.
- There is also a disagreement over which traits are the most important for an effective leader. 		This is an approach to the study of human personality. Trait theorists are primarily interested in the measurement of traits, which can be defined as habitual patterns of behavior, thought, and emotion.
